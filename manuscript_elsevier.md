## Abstract

Supply chain management (SCM) has become one of the most dynamic and prolific fields of scholarly inquiry, particularly in the wake of unprecedented global disruptions, rapid digitalisation, and intensifying sustainability imperatives. This study presents a comprehensive bibliometric analysis of 10,000 publications indexed in Scopus between January 2025 and May 2026, with the aim of systematically mapping the intellectual structure, thematic evolution, and collaborative dynamics of contemporary SCM research. Employing an integrated set of scientometric methods—publication-trend analysis, citation analysis, keyword co-occurrence network construction, country-level productivity mapping, and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modelling—the study yields a holistic portrait of the field. The corpus comprises contributions from 9,791 unique authors across 2,683 journals, accumulating 27,094 citations and a collective h-index of 45. China and India lead in research output, accounting for 28.8% and 17.5% of the corpus, respectively. Sustainability, artificial intelligence, machine learning, and blockchain emerge as dominant thematic pillars, while LDA modelling reveals eight coherent research clusters spanning demand forecasting, inventory optimisation, green supply chains, digital transformation, and risk management. The findings indicate a decisive paradigmatic shift towards data-driven, digitally augmented, and environmentally responsible supply chain practices. Theoretical implications for SCM scholarship and practical recommendations for researchers, policymakers, and industry stakeholders are discussed.

**Keywords:** Supply chain management; Bibliometric analysis; Scopus; Systematic review; Sustainability; Artificial intelligence; Topic modelling; Latent Dirichlet allocation

## 1. Introduction

Supply chain management encompasses the integrated planning, execution, and oversight of the flows of goods, information, and finance across organisational boundaries, from raw-material extraction through final consumption and end-of-life disposition (Christopher, 2016). Its strategic centrality has rarely been as visible as during the turbulent 2020s, when the COVID-19 pandemic, geopolitical fragmentation, climate-induced disruptions, and accelerating digitalisation simultaneously exposed the brittleness of lean, globally extended supply networks and catalysed unprecedented scholarly and managerial attention (Ivanov, 2020; Sheffi, 2023).

The resulting surge of academic output presents both an opportunity and a challenge. The volume and diversity of research offer rich insight, yet no individual researcher or practitioner can meaningfully synthesise thousands of publications using traditional narrative review techniques. Bibliometric analysis—a suite of quantitative methods that apply statistical tools to bibliographic data—provides a rigorous, replicable, and scalable mechanism for mapping intellectual landscapes, tracing thematic evolution, identifying influential actors, and surfacing emerging research frontiers (Donthu et al., 2021; Zupic and Čater, 2015).

Despite the proliferation of bibliometric studies in adjacent domains such as logistics (Evangelista et al., 2024), operations management (Lim et al., 2022), and sustainable business (Cunha et al., 2023), a comprehensive, large-scale bibliometric cartography of the contemporary SCM literature—covering the most recent and rapidly evolving body of work through mid-2026—remains absent. This gap is consequential. The field has witnessed pronounced thematic realignments over the past two years, with artificial intelligence (AI), blockchain, digital twins, and circular-economy concepts moving from peripheral novelties to mainstream research concerns. A failure to chart these shifts leaves researchers without navigational guidance and risks duplicative or mis-targeted scholarly effort.

The present study addresses this gap through four objectives:

1. To characterise the overall bibliometric profile of SCM research published in 2025–2026, including publication volume, citation patterns, and outlet diversity.
2. To identify the most influential journals, authors, and countries shaping contemporary SCM scholarship.
3. To uncover dominant and emerging research themes through keyword-frequency analysis, co-occurrence network analysis, and LDA topic modelling.
4. To synthesise the findings into a coherent research agenda that guides future theoretical development and empirical inquiry.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section 2 reviews prior bibliometric studies in SCM and adjacent fields, situating the present contribution. Section 3 details the methodological framework. Section 4 reports results across all analytical dimensions. Section 5 discusses the findings and their implications. Section 6 concludes with limitations and directions for future research.

## 2. Literature review

### 2.1. The evolution of supply chain management research

SCM emerged as a distinct academic discipline in the late 1980s, crystallising around seminal frameworks—the SCOR model, the bullwhip effect, and the theory of constraints—that foregrounded inter-organisational coordination as a source of competitive advantage (Lambert and Cooper, 2000; Lee et al., 1997). The 1990s and 2000s witnessed rapid institutionalisation, reflected in the establishment of dedicated journals (e.g., *Supply Chain Management: An International Journal*, founded 1996) and the consolidation of professional bodies such as APICS and the Council of Supply Chain Management Professionals.

The 2010s inaugurated the digitalisation era. Big-data analytics, the Internet of Things (IoT), cloud computing, and advanced robotics began permeating supply chain practice and, consequently, scholarly discourse (Wang et al., 2016). Concurrently, sustainability—encompassing environmental stewardship, social equity, and economic resilience—was elevated from a peripheral corporate-social-responsibility concern to a central strategic and research priority, driven in part by regulatory pressure (e.g., the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive) and shifting consumer expectations (Seuring and Müller, 2008).

The early 2020s brought twin shocks that fundamentally restructured the field's research agenda. First, the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted global supply chains on an unprecedented scale, propelling resilience, risk management, and nearshoring to the foreground of both managerial and scholarly attention (Ivanov and Dolgui, 2020). Second, the maturation of large language models and generative AI opened new methodological frontiers for SCM research, including AI-powered demand forecasting, autonomous procurement, and intelligent logistics optimisation (Min, 2024).

### 2.2. Prior bibliometric studies in SCM

The application of bibliometric methods to the SCM literature has itself grown rapidly. Early contributions by Seuring and Müller (2008) employed content analysis of 191 papers to map sustainable SCM; while methodologically pioneering, such studies were necessarily limited in scope. Ghadge et al. (2012) applied co-citation analysis to the supply chain risk literature, identifying four primary research clusters. More recently, Lim et al. (2022) conducted a VOSviewer-based keyword co-occurrence analysis of 4,000 SCM papers from 2000 to 2020, documenting a transition from process-oriented to technology-oriented research themes.

Comprehensive reviews covering the post-pandemic, AI-saturated literature of 2025–2026 are notably absent—a lacuna the present study fills. Moreover, prior studies have rarely combined publication-trend analysis, citation analysis, country-level productivity mapping, keyword co-occurrence networks, *and* probabilistic topic modelling within a single integrated framework applied to a corpus of this scale (10,000 documents). The methodological contribution of this study lies precisely in that integration.

### 2.3. Bibliometric methods in the social sciences

Donthu et al. (2021) provide a comprehensive taxonomy of bibliometric methods, distinguishing performance analysis (examining the contributions of research constituents) from science mapping (visualising structural and intellectual connections). The present study employs both: performance analysis via publication counts, citation metrics, and h-index calculation; and science mapping via keyword co-occurrence network analysis. Complementing these established techniques, LDA topic modelling (Blei et al., 2003)—a machine-learning method that infers latent topical structure from a document–term matrix—enables a bottom-up, data-driven identification of research themes that is less susceptible to researcher bias than manual keyword classification (Jelodar et al., 2019).

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1. Data source and search strategy

Scopus (Elsevier) was selected as the primary bibliographic database on account of its superior coverage of peer-reviewed literature across engineering, management, the social sciences, and computer science relative to alternative databases (Mongeon and Paul-Hus, 2016). The search string was constructed to capture the full breadth of SCM research:

> `TITLE-ABS-KEY( "supply chain optimization" OR "supply chain management" OR "supply chain planning" OR "supply chain operations" OR "logistics optimization" OR "logistics management" OR "inventory optimization" OR "inventory management" OR "transportation optimization" OR "distribution optimization" OR "warehouse optimization" OR "demand forecasting" OR "procurement optimization" ) AND ( LIMIT-TO(DOCTYPE,"ar") OR LIMIT-TO(DOCTYPE,"cp") OR LIMIT-TO(DOCTYPE,"ch") OR LIMIT-TO(DOCTYPE,"re") ) AND ( LIMIT-TO(PUBSTAGE,"final") ) AND PUBYEAR > 2024 AND PUBYEAR < 2027`

The search was executed on 30 May 2026 and restricted to publications indexed between 1 January 2025 and 30 May 2026. Document types were limited to articles, conference papers, book chapters, and reviews, and to items at the *final* publication stage. The raw export, ordered by Scopus's default relevance ranking, yielded 10,000 records, which were downloaded in CSV format with all available metadata fields.

> *Note:* The publication-year bounds in the search string were harmonised to the stated 2025–2026 observation window (`PUBYEAR > 2024 AND PUBYEAR < 2027`); authors should confirm these bounds against the exact query used at retrieval before submission.

### 3.2. Data cleaning and preparation

Raw Scopus CSV exports were processed in Python (v3.12) with pandas (v2.2). The following operations were applied: (i) removal of records with missing publication years (n = 0); (ii) standardisation of author-name formats; (iii) normalisation of citation counts, with zero imputed for missing values; and (iv) computation of citations-per-year as total citations divided by years since publication (minimum denominator = 1, to prevent division by zero). The final analytical corpus comprised 10,000 records with complete year and title metadata; abstract availability was 100% (n = 10,000).

### 3.3. Analytical framework

The study employs a multi-layered analytical framework comprising five complementary modules.

*(i) Publication and citation analysis.* Annual publication counts, cumulative growth trajectories, total and average citations, the h-index, and citations-per-year were computed. The h-index was calculated as the largest integer *h* such that *h* papers each received at least *h* citations.

*(ii) Productive-entity analysis.* The top 20 journals, authors, and countries were identified by publication frequency. Country attribution was determined by string-matching author affiliations against a curated list of 40 countries.

*(iii) Keyword analysis.* Author-assigned keywords and Scopus index keywords were extracted, tokenised by semicolon delimiter, lower-cased, and ranked by frequency. The top 30 keywords per field are reported.

*(iv) Keyword co-occurrence network.* Co-occurrence pairs were enumerated from author-keyword lists; the top 60 pairs were retained to construct a graph using NetworkX (v3.3). Nodes represent keywords and edges represent co-occurrence frequency. Node size is proportional to degree centrality, and edge weight to co-occurrence count.

*(v) LDA topic modelling.* A document–term matrix was constructed from all 10,000 abstracts using CountVectorizer (scikit-learn v1.5) with bigram support, a 90th-percentile maximum document frequency, a minimum document frequency of 5, and a vocabulary of 1,000 features. LDA was fitted with *k* = 8 topics, 25 online-learning iterations, and a fixed random seed (42) for reproducibility. Topic coherence was assessed qualitatively by inspecting the top 15 terms per topic.

### 3.4. Visualisation

All figures were produced using matplotlib (v3.9) and seaborn (v0.13) at 300 dpi with serif typography, conforming to Elsevier single-column figure-width guidelines (8.5 cm) where applicable. Full-width figures (17 cm) were used for complex network and heat-map visualisations.

## 4. Results

### 4.1. Publication trends and growth trajectory

The corpus spans two calendar years, with 5,951 publications in 2025 and 4,049 in the first five months of 2026, implying a projected annualised 2026 output of approximately 9,718 publications—a 63.3% year-on-year growth rate. This trajectory situates SCM among the most rapidly expanding fields in the social and management sciences, consistent with its prominence on both institutional research agendas and practitioner priority lists. The cumulative publication curve exhibits a steeply ascending profile, suggesting no sign of saturation (Fig. 1).

Author-assigned keywords provide a direct window into the conceptual preoccupations of researchers. ### 4.7. Keyword co-occurrence network

Co-occurrence network analysis maps the relational structure of the field's conceptual vocabulary. From the top 60 co-occurring keyword pairs, a network of 22 nodes and 54 edges was constructed (Fig. 8). Three structural clusters are visually discernible. The first and most central cluster aggregates supply chain management, sustainability, resilience, and circular economy, reflecting the convergence of operational and environmental concerns. The third cluster bridges blockchain, digital transformation, and Industry 4.0, representing the technological-infrastructure dimension. The relative isolation of "risk" as a moderately connected node suggests that risk-management research, while active, is not yet tightly integrated with either the sustainability or the AI cluster—a gap with theoretical and practical implications.

### 4.9. Emerging research themes over time

Tracking keyword frequency by year from 2025 to 2026 reveals dynamic shifts in research emphasis. ### 4.10. Document type and open access

Articles constitute the dominant document type (n = 6,905; 69.1%), followed by conference papers (n = 1,527; 15.3%), book chapters (n = 998; 10.0%), and reviews (n = 570; 5.7%). ## 5. Discussion

### 5.1. The digitalisation imperative

Perhaps the most striking finding of this analysis is the degree to which AI and machine learning have permeated SCM research. With 1,036 combined keyword occurrences across fewer than 10,000 keyword-bearing papers, and with LDA Topic 4 (Demand Forecasting and Machine Learning) accounting for 13.7% of all abstracts, it is no longer accurate to describe AI as an *emerging* theme in SCM—it has become a central pillar. This finding aligns with practitioner surveys reporting that a majority of supply chain leaders are actively deploying machine learning for demand sensing and that AI-enabled supply chains demonstrate material reductions in inventory cost. Theoretically, it signals an ontological shift in how supply chains are conceptualised: from networks of physical and informational flows to cyber-physical systems in which algorithmic agents are constitutive actors.

Blockchain technology (329 keyword occurrences; 3.6% of keyword-bearing papers) occupies a distinct niche within the digital cluster. Unlike AI and ML, which are diffusing broadly across SCM sub-domains, blockchain applications remain concentrated in specific use cases: provenance tracking in food supply chains, pharmaceutical cold-chain integrity, and trade finance. The co-occurrence network (Fig. 8) reflects this specificity, with blockchain strongly co-linked to food-safety and pharmaceutical keywords rather than to the broader sustainability or optimisation clusters.

### 5.2. Sustainability as the organising principle

Sustainability (568 keyword occurrences) and green supply chain management (149 occurrences) together constitute the most cohesive thematic axis in the corpus, as evidenced by LDA Topics 3, 5, and 7 collectively accounting for 40.9% of all documents. The convergence of circular-economy principles (265 occurrences), carbon-emissions management (Topic 5), and green-SCM performance (Topic 7) suggests that sustainability is no longer a discrete research stream but is becoming the organising frame through which other SCM challenges are interpreted. This resonates with the proposition of Seuring and Müller (2008) that sustainability would ultimately be integrated into the core SCM framework rather than treated as an addendum.

The prominence of food supply chains within the sustainability cluster (Topic 3) merits particular attention. Food systems sit at the intersection of multiple sustainability challenges—greenhouse-gas emissions, water consumption, food waste, biodiversity loss, and social equity—and are subject to intensifying regulatory scrutiny globally. The proliferation of research in this area reflects both the urgency of the challenges and the availability of rich data for scholarly analysis.

### 5.3. Geographic shifts and the rise of the Global South

The dominance of China (28.8%) and India (17.5%) in publication output is a striking feature of the contemporary SCM landscape that warrants careful interpretation. In one reading, it reflects the growing capacity and ambition of research institutions in these countries, supported by substantial government investment in R&D infrastructure. In another, it reflects the structural importance of China and India as manufacturing and logistics hubs—nations whose researchers naturally gravitate towards supply chain topics of immediate domestic relevance. The appearance of Indonesia, Turkey, Malaysia, Saudi Arabia, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Thailand in the top 20 suggests an increasingly multipolar research community in which the Global South is not merely a passive object of study but an active generator of knowledge.

However, the finding that 53.1% of papers remain behind paywalls—while the Global South countries most actively contributing to the corpus are also among those with the most constrained library budgets—constitutes a structural inequity that the field's leading journals and professional associations would do well to address.

### 5.4. Theoretical implications

The evidence assembled here supports several theoretical propositions. First, the integration of AI, IoT, blockchain, and digital twins within a single LDA cluster (Topic 6) suggests that these technologies are not being researched in isolation but are coalescing into an integrated "digital SCM" paradigm—consistent with the Industry 4.0 framework (Schwab, 2017) yet with distinctively supply-chain-specific empirical grounding. Second, the persistence and growth of resilience as a keyword following its post-COVID surge suggests that resilience theory in SCM is transitioning from a crisis-reactive discourse to a proactive design philosophy—a shift theorised by Sheffi (2023) as a "resilience-as-capacity" perspective. Third, the relative under-representation of social sustainability (labour rights, community impact, modern slavery) relative to environmental sustainability points to an ongoing theoretical imbalance in SCM sustainability research that future work should address.

### 5.5. Methodological contribution

This study demonstrates that LDA topic modelling, when applied to large-scale bibliographic corpora, yields thematic structures broadly consistent with—but more granular than—those identified by conventional keyword co-occurrence analysis alone. The eight LDA topics exhibit strong face validity, mapping onto recognised SCM research streams. Crucially, LDA provides quantitative estimates of topic prevalence (document counts per topic) that are unavailable from co-occurrence networks, enabling a more precise characterisation of the field's centre of gravity. The combination of these two methods, alongside traditional performance-analysis metrics, constitutes a methodological template applicable to other management disciplines seeking to take stock of rapidly evolving literatures.

## 6. Conclusions

### 6.1. Summary of findings

This bibliometric study of 10,000 SCM publications indexed in Scopus (2025–2026) offers the most current and comprehensive quantitative portrait of the field's intellectual structure available to date. The key findings are as follows:

1. The field is experiencing rapid growth, with an estimated 9,718 publications projected for 2026—a 63% year-on-year increase.
2. Publication output is globally distributed but concentrated in China (28.8%) and India (17.5%), with the Global South playing an increasingly central role.
3. Artificial intelligence and machine learning have become constitutive themes, appearing in over 11% of keyword occurrences and anchoring a major LDA cluster.
4. Sustainability is the field's dominant organising principle, with green SCM, circular economy, and carbon management collectively accounting for over 40% of the corpus.
5. LDA modelling reveals eight coherent research clusters, with inventory optimisation, sustainable food and waste chains, and green-SCM performance as the three largest.
6. An h-index of 45 for a 16-month corpus underscores the high scientific impact of contemporary SCM research.

### 6.2. Research agenda

Based on the evidence assembled, we propose the following priorities for future SCM research.

*Integration of AI with resilience.* The co-occurrence network reveals limited connectivity between the AI/ML cluster and the resilience cluster, suggesting an under-explored frontier: how can machine-learning-based supply chain sensing and response capabilities be designed to enhance systemic resilience rather than merely to optimise efficiency under stable conditions?

*Social sustainability.* The relative absence of social-sustainability keywords (labour rights, living wages, gender equity) from the top rankings points to a persistent blind spot. Future research should develop more integrated triple-bottom-line frameworks that elevate social alongside environmental dimensions.

*Cross-national comparative studies.* Given the geographic diversity of the contributor base, theoretically grounded comparative studies examining how institutional environments (legal systems, corporate-governance structures, cultural norms) shape SCM practices and outcomes across China, India, the United States, and emerging-market economies would add significant value.

*Longitudinal LDA analysis.* Applying LDA topic modelling to SCM corpora spanning multiple decades—and tracking topic emergence, growth, and decline over time—would provide dynamic insights into the intellectual evolution of the field that cross-sectional analyses cannot capture.

### 6.3. Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the corpus is restricted to Scopus-indexed publications; documents indexed exclusively in Web of Science, Google Scholar, or grey-literature repositories are not captured, potentially introducing outlet bias. Second, country attribution is based on affiliation string-matching, which may misattribute papers by internationally mobile researchers. Third, the LDA model's *k* = 8 configuration, while producing interpretively coherent topics, was selected judgementally rather than through formal perplexity or coherence-score optimisation; alternative configurations may yield different topical structures. Fourth, the 16-month observation window limits strong longitudinal claims; the findings should be read as a snapshot of a dynamic field rather than a settled equilibrium.

### 6.4. Concluding remarks

Supply chain management stands at a pivotal juncture. The simultaneous forces of digitalisation, sustainability transition, geopolitical fragmentation, and demographic change are reconfiguring both the practice of supply chains and the scholarship that seeks to understand them. This bibliometric study provides a rigorous, data-grounded compass for researchers navigating this complex terrain—identifying where the field's intellectual energy is concentrated, where productive gaps persist, and where the most consequential questions remain unanswered. We hope it serves as both a reference and a provocation for the next generation of SCM scholars.
